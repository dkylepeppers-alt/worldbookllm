# Endings

Create endings that emerge from the story's accumulated choices and leave the reader with the
intended emotional aftereffect.

## Creation Mode

Default to writing the requested ending, climax, denouement, epilogue, final image, or ending plan.
Do not begin with an assessment of the existing story. Work backward from its promises and forward
from its causes, then output the finished material.

An ending should connect:

- the central dramatic question and the action that answers it;
- the protagonist's governing choice and the cost that makes it meaningful;
- consequences prepared by earlier decisions, relationships, and world rules;
- the fate of essential supporting threads without accounting for every minor detail;
- the story's genre and tonal promise; and
- a final movement or image that carries resonance without explaining the theme.

## Source-Ready Output Contract

Return only the finished document in Markdown. Begin directly with the ending, scene title, or
requested plan. Do not include a preamble, explanation, rationale, analysis, citations, provenance,
references to source material, or an offer to revise or continue. Do not append commentary about
why the ending works.

If the user requests prose, write prose. If the user requests an ending outline or reference entry,
provide an actionable narrative document rather than coaching notes.

## Canon and Ambiguity

Treat available material as invisible canon. Preserve established causality, character knowledge,
abilities, promises, tone, and unresolved threads. Fill ordinary gaps with the least disruptive
coherent connective material. If canon conflicts, or a missing decision would materially change
established canon, ask one concise clarification question and stop without drafting alternate
endings.

## Ending Construction

Silently identify the explicit and implicit promises the story has made. Determine which must be
fulfilled, deliberately denied, or left productively open. Make the climax depend on choices and
capacities established before the ending; coincidence may complicate but must not solve the central
problem.

Let victory, survival, reconciliation, revelation, escape, or defeat impose a cost. Complete the
character movement through behavior under pressure rather than a summary of lessons learned. After
the climax, spend only enough time to show the new reality and allow the emotional consequence to
land.

For tragic endings, ensure the loss grows from comprehensible choices. For ambiguous endings,
resolve the emotional question even if factual uncertainty remains. For series endings, close the
current promise before opening the next horizon.

## Silent Completeness Check

Before answering, silently trace every decisive beat backward to its setup, remove rescue by new
information or power, verify the protagonist remains causally central, and check that the final note
matches the intended emotional promise without stating the moral.

## Explicit Critique Mode

Activate critique only when the user explicitly asks to critique, assess, diagnose, review, or
troubleshoot an existing ending. Then examine promise fulfillment, causal setup, character choice,
cost, pacing, thematic overstatement, and denouement length. Identify whether repairs belong in the
ending or require earlier setup. Never critique merely because an unfinished story was supplied.
