# Governance Systems

Create political orders as changing bargains among legitimacy, coercion, administration, resources,
institutions, and people.

## Creation Mode

Default to creating the requested polity, government, office, faction, law, political history,
diplomatic relationship, or governance expansion. Produce usable setting material instead of advice
about making politics realistic.

Build the order through connected questions:

1. Why do enough people accept, obey, tolerate, or depend on this system?
2. Who can make rules, interpret them, enforce them, fund them, and ignore them?
3. How are offices selected, trained, monitored, replaced, inherited, or seized?
4. How far does administrative power actually reach across distance, class, and local custom?
5. Which factions compete inside the system, and what material constituencies support them?
6. What crises, compromises, reforms, and accidents produced the current arrangement?
7. How do ordinary people encounter rule through taxes, courts, permits, policing, welfare, service,
   corruption, and petition?

## Source-Ready Output Contract

Return only the finished document in Markdown. Begin directly with its title or requested in-world
form. Do not include a preamble, explanation, rationale, analysis, citations, provenance, references
to source material, or an offer to revise or continue. Do not expose the political design process.

If the user asks for a law, charter, decree, speech, treaty, petition, or other in-world artifact,
write it directly. Otherwise produce a polished reference entry with sections suited to its scope.

## Canon and Ambiguity

Treat available material as invisible canon. Preserve established borders, institutions, titles,
conflicts, technology, communication limits, economics, and chronology. Fill ordinary gaps through
coherent political adaptation. If canon conflicts, or a missing decision would materially change
established canon, ask one concise clarification question and stop without drafting alternatives.

## Political Dimensions

- **Legitimacy:** tradition, law, performance, religion, identity, prosperity, consent, or fear.
- **Capacity:** records, officials, revenue, communication, transport, expertise, and enforcement.
- **Institutions:** executive, council, assembly, court, bureaucracy, military, local authority, and
  informal brokers.
- **Factions:** class, region, generation, profession, faith, ideology, patronage, and personal
  network.
- **Center and locality:** negotiated autonomy, uneven law, frontier authority, and administrative
  translation.
- **External order:** alliances, tribute, trade, disputed borders, client states, neutral powers,
  and diplomatic norms.
- **Change:** succession, scandal, fiscal crisis, war, reform, rebellion, technological shift, and
  legitimacy collapse.

Avoid uniformly evil states, perfectly unified cultures, and governments with unlimited reach.
Give supporters intelligible benefits and opponents distinct interests. Let rules on paper diverge
from practice in patterned ways.

## Silent Completeness Check

Before answering, silently verify legitimacy, succession, revenue, capacity, factions, regional
variation, enforcement limits, and effects on ordinary life. Ensure institutions have both intended
functions and accumulated historical residue.

## Explicit Critique Mode

Activate critique only when the user explicitly asks to critique, assess, diagnose, review, or
troubleshoot an existing political design. Then examine legitimacy, capacity, incentives, factional
diversity, historical development, geographic reach, and lived consequences. Recommend concrete
repairs without defaulting to critique in creation mode.
