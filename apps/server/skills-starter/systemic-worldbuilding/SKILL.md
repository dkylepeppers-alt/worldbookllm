# Systemic Worldbuilding

Create setting material by following change through connected systems until it becomes visible in
institutions, conflicts, habits, language, and ordinary choices.

## Creation Mode

Default to creating the requested world expansion, consequence map, alternate history, institution,
culture, or reference entry. Perform systemic analysis silently, then output the resulting world
material rather than a discussion of possibilities.

Begin with a divergence or governing condition and trace:

1. **First-order effects:** direct capabilities, limits, dangers, costs, and immediate winners or
   losers.
2. **Second-order adaptations:** institutions, markets, laws, professions, infrastructure,
   resistance, and strategic responses.
3. **Third-order normalization:** family patterns, status, language, ethics, art, identity, customs,
   and assumptions of people born into the changed world.
4. **Feedback:** adaptations that amplify, constrain, redirect, or destabilize the original change.
5. **Uneven experience:** differences across class, region, generation, profession, faith, species,
   and access.

## Source-Ready Output Contract

Return only the finished document in Markdown. Begin directly with its title or requested in-world
form. Do not include a preamble, explanation, rationale, analysis, citations, provenance, references
to source material, an exposed consequence exercise, or an offer to revise or continue. The response
must read as an original world source, not notes about constructing one.

## Canon and Ambiguity

Treat available material as invisible canon. Preserve established divergence points, rules, limits,
history, geography, institutions, and tone. Fill ordinary gaps by tracing consequences. If canon
conflicts, or a missing decision would materially change established canon, ask one concise
clarification question and stop without drafting multiple models.

## Consequence Domains

Trace only relevant domains, but connect at least several of them:

- ecology, resources, settlement, and infrastructure;
- production, labor, trade, ownership, and inequality;
- governance, law, warfare, diplomacy, and surveillance;
- kinship, education, religion, medicine, and social status;
- knowledge, communication, art, entertainment, and language;
- identity, ethics, taboo, aspiration, and everyday etiquette.

For every advantage, identify a cost or excluded group. For every restriction, identify adaptation,
evasion, or a beneficiary. Distinguish what the innovation makes possible from what institutions
allow people to access. Let old systems survive in altered forms rather than assuming total replacement.

## Silent Completeness Check

Before answering, silently follow major consequences to at least third order, connect changes across
domains, identify winners and losers, vary responses among populations, test feedback loops, and
translate abstractions into things characters notice and do. Remove chains that are logical but
irrelevant to the requested document.

## Explicit Critique Mode

Activate critique only when the user explicitly asks to critique, assess, diagnose, review, or
troubleshoot existing systemic worldbuilding. Then locate stopped consequence chains, uniform
responses, missing material constraints, absent feedback, or changes that never reach daily life.
Recommend specific extensions without defaulting to critique during creation.
