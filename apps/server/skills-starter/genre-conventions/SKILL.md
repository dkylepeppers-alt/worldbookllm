# Genre Conventions

Create genre material by designing for the experience the reader expects, not by mechanically
inserting a checklist of familiar props.

## Creation Mode

Default to creating the requested premise, world entry, character, conflict, scene, sequence, or
genre expansion. Determine the primary emotional promise and make every major choice support it.
Do not list conventions or explain genre theory in the response.

Useful elemental promises include:

- **Wonder:** encounter with the extraordinary and expansion of possibility.
- **Horror:** mounting dread, vulnerability, transgression, and costly confrontation.
- **Mystery:** compelling uncertainty followed by earned understanding.
- **Thriller:** escalating danger, compressed choices, and pursuit under pressure.
- **Adventure:** hazardous movement toward a concrete objective through changing environments.
- **Romance:** growing intimacy tested by meaningful internal and external barriers.
- **Drama:** consequential human choices within relationships and social constraints.
- **Comedy:** tension released through incongruity, reversal, exposure, and social truth.
- **Tragedy:** understandable choices narrowing toward irreversible loss.
- **Crime:** desire, transgression, investigation, loyalty, power, and consequence.
- **Western or frontier:** contested order, distance, extraction, settlement, and self-made law.

Adapt these promises to the user's named genre and canon rather than forcing a fixed taxonomy.

## Source-Ready Output Contract

Return only the finished document in Markdown. Begin directly with its title or requested narrative
form. Do not include a preamble, explanation, rationale, analysis, citations, provenance, references
to source material, a convention checklist, or an offer to revise or continue. Never announce the
genre techniques being used.

## Canon and Ambiguity

Treat available material as invisible canon. Preserve established tone, rules, chronology,
characters, and genre commitments. Fill ordinary gaps with inventions that reinforce the primary
promise. If canon conflicts, or a missing decision would materially change established canon, ask
one concise clarification question and stop without drafting alternatives.

## Genre Synthesis

Silently define the primary genre promise, the emotional progression, and the decisive payoff. Give
characters vulnerabilities and desires that make them susceptible to that experience. Make setting
rules generate the appropriate pressures. Choose imagery, pacing, information flow, and consequences
that reinforce the promise.

When combining genres, keep one primary. A secondary genre may complicate, intensify, or provide
contrast, but it must not repeatedly cancel the primary experience. Humor can deepen attachment in
horror but should not dissolve every threat; mystery can structure romance but should not prevent
the relationship from progressing.

Use conventions as contracts that can be fulfilled unexpectedly. Surprise the path, cost, agent,
or meaning while still delivering the promised emotional event.

## Silent Completeness Check

Before answering, silently verify that character, setting, conflict, pacing, and payoff point toward
the same primary experience; that secondary genres have a defined role; and that recognizable
elements arise from this specific canon rather than generic genre furniture.

## Explicit Critique Mode

Activate critique only when the user explicitly asks to critique, assess, diagnose, review, or
troubleshoot existing material. Then identify the apparent genre promise, test whether major
elements deliver it, distinguish productive subversion from accidental omission, and recommend
specific alignment changes. Do not critique merely because genre material was supplied.
